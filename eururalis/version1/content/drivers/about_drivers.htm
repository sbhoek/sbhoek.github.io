Driving forces are externally working forces exerting influence on the European rural area and its functions for society. They are relatively exogenous, such as the global economy or global change processes. Various forces act synchronously and interact. Their impact will vary through time and from region to region. Their future role is hard to assess: some of them are principally hard to predict, others can be quantified between reasonable limits. The scenario approach tries to specify consistent story lines that distinguish between the nature, rate and extent of various driving forces We distinguish:

  • natural forces affecting natural or cultural ecosystems, such as natural or man-induced climate change or sea level rise. Major disasters, such as earthquakes, flooding or epidemic diseases could be added but are hard to include. Such incidents are hard to predict in nature, size, region or impact. Expectations on Global Change however have scientifically and politically converged.


  • geopolitical change: it can be expected that the effects of the EU enlargement will continue while further expansion is possible, in some scenarios. International agreements (WTO or other) seem to gain momentum (e.g. scenario B1), in others their role is much more modest.


  • Demographic change: growth, decline of populations, major migration flows, and in case of declining population growth the ageing of people. Specific for rural areas the flow from rural to urban areas is relevant.


  • World economy is a powerful determinant of changes in agriculture and other sectors (industry, services). Supply and demand determine trade. For agriculture the changes in food demands are important.


  • Technology is a major driver. It causes huge and fast shifts, in agriculture via higher efficiency in production (per farm, per hectare, per hour), the pull from other economic sectors for capital, labour, space and how non-economic activities of people can be realized (mobility of persons, recreation patterns). Completely new inventions are hard to predict, once their relevance has been shown dissemination of technologies can be assumed.


  • Consumer patterns encompass al kinds of human preferences and behaviour .This varies from preferences in diet, attitude towards animal welfare, nature conservation to the acceptance of government intervention.
