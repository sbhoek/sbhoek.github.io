Depending on the scenario, in the period 2000-2030 Europe’s population will grow slightly (A1, B1) will remain constant (A2) or will decrease (B2).


In all scenario’s the average age of the population will sharply rise.


Larger regional differences exist: in Southern and Eastern Europe the population will most probably decrease.


In all scenarios the rural population is decreasing.


Population size and structure are determined by three fundamental demographic processes fertility, mortality and migration. For each of these three variables scenario specific assumptions have been made (Table 1). For the (extended) EU it is assumed that the fertility rate is more or less linked with economic growth. Life expectancy in Western European countries is considered to be high and slowly rising in all scenarios. The speed of converging in other EU-countries to this level of life expectancy is also depending on the economic growth rate in these countries. The same holds more or less for the migration rate.

It shows that in the A1 scenario the population of the EU-25 will grow with approx. 8% from 453 to 491 million people in 2030. Also in the B1 scenario their will be a increase in population, but with 5% to 476. In the A2 scenario the total population of EU-25 will increase slightly, but the population size in the 10 new member states will decrease 12%. In the B2 scenario the population will decrease 5% to 430 million inhabitants. In all scenarios the “grey pressure” (age 65 and over as a ratio of 15-64 years) will sharply increase. For the EU-15 this will be an increase from 25% in 2000 to 38% in 2030 in the A1 scenario to almost 45% in the B2-scenario.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Compared to developing countries Europese population stagnates in all scenarios

The rural population is decreasing in all scenarios, by 23 to 33%. This is a very strong decline, stronger then in the period 1970-2000. This is partly caused by a decreasing total population and by an increasing urbanization. The share of rural population in the total population is however harder to predict then the total population itself. Moreover, the definition of “rural population” is strongly differing from country to country. More insight to the extent, causes and effects of the shrinking population is needed.
