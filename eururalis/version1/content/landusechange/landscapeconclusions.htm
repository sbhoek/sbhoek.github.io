Non-linear changes in arable land area lead to irreversible changes in the rural landscape



In most accession countries, a non-linear change in the demand for non-irrigated arable land is found in the B1 scenario. Typically, the arable land area increases until 2010 followed by a decrease in area until 2030. This is due to changes in agricultural policies after 2010: until 2010 the accession countries will benefit from European agricultural policy and production quota. This scenario, however, implies that production quota are abolished which will result in a stronger influence of liberalization after 2010. In the Czech Republic, the increase of arable land comes at the cost of a decrease in nature area in the first 10 years, followed by a decrease of arable land area and the development of new nature on abandoned land in the last 20 years. However, nature will not return automatically at the locations where it is lost during the first ten years. During the first ten years it is mainly the small patches of nature in the main agricultural area that are lost (purple circle in maps), while new nature develops on abandoned marginal lands adjacent to existing nature areas (blue circle). This pathway of change has important, irreversible consequences for the rural area and landscape diversity.





Agricultural change influences the landscape

The demand for agricultural land is a result of the interaction between productivity improvement, management change and changes in imports and exports. In the A1 scenario this leads to a decreasing demand for arable land and pasture while the A2 scenario faces a modest expansion of the agricultural area, especially during the first decade. These differences between scenarios are reflected in the landscape patterns of 2030. In the A2 scenario a lot of small patches of nature have been replaced by agricultural uses. Some of the margins of larger nature areas have been converted to agriculture as well. These conversions have important consequences for the landscape and associate biodiversity and scenic values.


These results are counterintuitive to the nature protection assumptions defined in the storyline of the A2 scenario: protection of local patches of nature is assumed to be more important in the A2 scenario as compared to the A1 scenario. However, demand for land has a larger effect on the landscape than the intention to protect patches of nature.


The scenarios conditions also have other effects on landscape diversity: the trend to optimize land use configurations in the A1 scenario leads to a conversion of relatively marginal croplands in the hilly and mountainous areas to grasslands and a concentration of arable lands in the most productive regions.

        
 

Nature development patterns depend on spatial policies

In B1 and B2, serious efforts are undertaken to protect and develop natural areas. However, the scenarios differ in spatial policies leading to different nature protection schemes. In B1, the policy aims at strengthening the Natura2000 network. This implies that, besides the protection of existing natural areas, abandoned agricultural areas are actively transformed into buffer zones and ecological corridors at national and international scale.


In B2, on the contrary, there are no efforts to develop a European level ecological structure. In this scenario, nature development is stimulated mainly in the agricultural landscapes.


The effect for landscape structure can clearly be seen in Finland. In both scenarios the natural areas increase approximately 1%. In B1, large, connected natural areas have been developed in 2030, while the land use pattern in the B2 scenario looks more variable and patchy. In this scenario most new nature is located adjacent or within the main agricultural areas. These different patterns of nature development have different consequences for biodiversity. While the decrease in disturbance of the large nature areas in the B1 scenario will benefit biodiversity in nature reserves, the expansion of nature within the agricultural areas in the B2 scenario will benefit agro-biodiversity.


  
 

Land abandonment leads to new natural areas

Italy faces a large abandonment of agricultural land in both the B1 and B2 scenario. This provides possibilities for the development of nature, either spontaneous through regrowth on abandoned fields or through active management. In the B1 and B2 scenarios serious efforts are undertaken to protect and develop natural areas. However, the scenarios differ in spatial policies leading to different nature protection schemes. In B1, the policy aims at strengthening the Natura2000 network. This implies that, besides the protection of existing natural areas, abandoned agricultural areas are actively managed to create buffer zones and ecological corridors at the national and international scale. In B2, on the contrary, there are no efforts undertaken to organize a European level ecological structure. In this scenario, nature development is stimulated mainly in the agricultural landscapes. The potential impact of these different spatial policies is clearly visible in parts of Italy. The regions where new nature is found are comparable: in both scenarios the relatively marginal lands are taken out of agricultural production. New natural areas develop as continuous areas directly adjacent to existing areas in the B1 scenario, strengthening the existing and new nature reserves. In the B2 scenario, new natural areas are more patchy and mixed with agricultural areas in such a way that it strengthens the historic landscape structure of this region.

A1 and A2 show growth of the city as well as the growth of its suburbs: sprawled urban growth all over the area around Katowice. The B1 policies favor compact urbanization resulting in stronger urban growth in existing urban areas and less urban sprawl. In B2 there is just a little urban growth due to the decreasing population and the lower economic growth rates. In the maps the effects of the different scenarios on nature development are recognizable as well; in A1 nature is lost due to urbanization but compensated by new natural areas on former agricultural land; in A2 small patches of nature disappear due to the expansion of agricultural area during the period 2000-2010; due to abandonment of agricultural land after 2010 some new nature is found adjacent to exiting natural areas; in B1 some of the natural areas North of Katowice belong to the Natura2000 network and are strengthened while in the B2 scenario some new patches of nature develop within the agricultural areas.

In the A1 scenario, growth of built-up area is highest due to high growth of population, GDP and industry/ service sectors. Adjacent to existing urban areas large new urban areas appear. However, at the same time strong urban sprawl and developments in small towns and villages throughout the area can be observed (e.g., due to the construction of second houses). In the A2 scenario, the urban development pattern is sprawled, just as in A1. Because the amount of built-up area growth is much lower than in the A1 scenario, growth of large cities is less striking.
Although the amount of built-up area growth in the B1 scenario is lower than in A1 and A2, large hot-spots of urbanization directly adjacent to the large urban centers like the Ruhrgebiet (Germany) and Randstad (The Netherlands) can be seen. This urbanization pattern is a result of the spatial policies aiming at compact urbanization assumed for this scenario. Sprawl and development of small towns and villages is much less pronounced in this scenario. In B2, growth of built-up area is low due to low population growth and low economic growth.

In the A1 scenario large parts of Europe are faced with a decreasing demand for arable land and pasture due to increasing productivity and import such as here in the north-eastern part of Poland. This results in large areas of abandoned agricultural land. Although nature protection is no important issue in A1, the decrease of agricultural area has positive consequences on natural values. Local patches of nature in the agricultural areas face less pressure from agriculture. Due to spontaneous development of nature on abandoned farmland the total nature area increases. The A2 scenario includes formally a policy to protect local patches of nature, but the demand for agricultural area is higher in A2, and for that reason this nature protection policy cannot be appropriately maintained.

Scenario conditions result in large differences in demand for agricultural and residential/industrial lands. These demands have large impacts on the landscape, as shown for southern Greece. Part of the abandoned farmlands will, after some time, be converted into nature, either due to regrowth of natural vegetations or through active management. In the A1 scenario land use dynamics are high: Athens large urban expansion and the abandonment of large areas of agricultural land results in an impressive increase of natural areas. In the B2 scenario urban growth is almost absent and land abandonment is small. Therefore, in B2 the dynamics of land use change are relatively small and the current landscape will not change dramatically.



The northern part of Italy has a sharp gradient from the Alps to the agricultural area of the Po delta. The mountain environment is classified as a Less Favoured Area (LFA) in which farmers receive compensation. Without this compensation arable land from the LFA regions would, most likely, first be abandoned due to the relative poor conditions for agriculture. Due to the compensations land abandonment takes place in the agricultural regions outside the LFA regions, especially on the footslopes of the mountains, just outside the LFA region. In areas surrounding Milan and Turin urban expansion takes over much arable land. The sensitivity of the land allocation to variations in LFA policies was tested by simulation of a policy variant. In this simulation a reduced compensation of farmers in LFA’s was assumed. Then leads to more land abandonment inside the LFA’s and less in the areas classified as ‘hot-spots’ of land abandonment outside the LFA. This nicely illustrates the possible effect of spatial policies on landscape patterns.



In the B2 scenario we find opposing trends in land use. While Portugal faces a considerable abandonment of agricultural lands similar to other EU15 countries, the accession countries (an example for the Baltic countries is shown) often find an expansion of the agricultural area during the first decade. Some land abandonment occurs after 2010. However, this does not replace the loss of small natural patches during the first decade. Land is abandoned in the more marginal areas.





In both the A2 and B2 scenarios patches of nature within the agricultural areas are protected. In practice, however, protection is not always possible due to high land requirements of agriculture. Especially in the A2 scenario the demands for expansion of the agricultural area are high compared to the other scenarios. This has important effects for the landscape, as is illustrated for part of the UK (green: natural areas; red: natural areas converted to other land use types). During the first years, small, local patches of nature disappear, followed by the edges of larger natural areas. In the B2 scenario demand for agricultural area is less and natural areas are generally conserved.
