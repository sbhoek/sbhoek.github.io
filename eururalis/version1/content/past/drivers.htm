Manifest driving forces over the past 30 years comprise economical globalization, geopolitical change, such as the EU formation, new technologies, demographic growth and consumption patterns. They were decisive in land use change.


Driving forces are symbolised by the roots of the tree. Their relative importance varies depending on a certain world vision (scenario), as symbolised by the scenarios A1, B1, A2 and B2.

 

what are drivers?

Drivers are relatively externally working forces exerting influence on the European rural area. They act synchronously and interact with each other. Their impact varies through time and from region to region. We distinguish :

  • Natural forces
  • Geopolitical change
  • Demographic change
  • World economy
  • Technology
  • Consumer patterns

 

In recent decades all categories have been at work in Europe. Some seem to have had minor impacts (climate change so far; Others had a quite contrasting course than expected for the future (demography)), others such as the EU-formation and expansion, a rapidly developing globalization or the strong influence of new technologies seem to have had an influence that has not yet ended.

 

                                            

climate

The average temperature in Europe has increased by 0,95 oC in the last 100 years

From 1850 to1980 glaciers in the Alps lost about one third of their area and half of their mass

Climate change increased the length of the growing season by 10 days between 1962 and 1995

Changes in temperature and precipitation differ over Europe. The last decade northern Europe got considerably more precipitation, southern Europe considerably less. The warming was highest in NW Russia and the Iberian Peninsula


Extreme events seem to occur more frequently




Due to increased emissions of greenhouse gases (carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, CFC's and HCFC's) since pre-industrial times atmospheric concentrations increased with 34 %. An acceleration in greenhouse gas emissions can be observed since 1950.


Climate change, visible in raised temperatures and change in precipitation patterns has been documented. The global temperature has increased by 0.7 +/- 0.2 centigrade over the last century, the 1990's were the warmest decade in the observed records. Europe has warmed somewhat more than the global average, with a 0.95 centigrade increase since 1900. Winter temperatures increased more than summer temperatures. The warming was highest in NW Russia and the Iberian Peninsula,


The effect of warming had strong impacts on glaciers in Europe. In eight out of nine European glacier regions a strong retreat has been observed: about one third of the area and one half of their mass vanished between 1850 and 1980. Since then 20-30 % of the remainder was lost. Rainfall patterns in Europe have not been documented as well as temperature. Recent observations for the last decade show contrasting patterns: northern Europa was 10-40 % wetter, southern Europe up to 20 % drier than the average for the century

 

Source: EEA (European Environment Agency), 2004. Impacts of Europe’s changing climate. A indicator-based assessment. EEA Report No 2/2004, Copenhagen, Denmark.



population

Over the last three centuries Europes population has grown tremendously

Ageing of the population has set in strongly since the sixties, mainly due to improvements in life expectancy, in combination with smaller families. The small disruptions in the 1980s are the effect of World War I. Sweden, Italy, United Kingdom and Belgium have the oldest population, while –in general- the Central and Eastern European countries have the youngest population.



Ireland, Greece and Netherlands show a strong increase (over 20%) in population in the period 1970-2000, while the population of Hungary and Bulgaria declined in the same period.

CAP

The Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) primarily focused on food security, low food prices and higher incomes for farmers. Later this scope was widened to rural development, social, environmental and ecological goals. Compliance to international trade agreements (WTO) is strived for. Complementary policy fields were developed for environment, nature conservation and restoration and water management.


Mansholt was commissioner for Agriculture (from 1958 untill 1972) and was among others responsible for the creation of the CAP. While he was responsible for the successes of the CAP, he also was fully aware of the drawbacks. His thinking was strongly influenced by the “Club of Rome” and he tried to reverse the negative aspects of the CAP. For a brief period in 1972 he also was president of the European Commission.


At the start of the EU the primary challenges for the agricultural sector were to prevent hunger, to provide cheap food and to raise incomes of farmers. Measures were market protection (tariff walls), export subsidies, price support, research, education, subsidies for fertilizers etcetera. Successes were amazingly fast, changes were massive and even brought unexpected impacts: surpluses of wheat, milk, meat, environmental and ecological problems as well as concerns on regional economies and socio-cultural assets due to massive labour expulsion. Farmers in less favoured areas were supported separately. Concurrently worries emerged on the size of the EU budget spent to agriculture / rural areas (yearly budget nowdays is 40 Billion Euro's) and the disrupting effects on international trade as was felt by developing countries. A major challenge is to adjust the CAP (Common Agricultural Policy) to agreements for international trade in WTO context (World Trade Organisation). EU's latest enlargement by 10 new member states with a relative high number of farmers brought new challenges for the CAP.


EU's policy on agriculture and rural areas reflects the above developments. Since the days of Mansholt various minor and major adjustments have been made or set in motion. Quota were the answer on overproduction, environmental and ecological problems were addressed and in successive adjustments a shift was realised from production support to farmers income support. Broadening of targets included the shift in attention towards rural development (the so-called 2nd Pillar of the CAP; for which a growth in budget from 5 to 10 % of the CAP budget was realised), where broader economic, social, environmental and ecological goals are combined. Successive CAP reforms aimed at these goals (McSharry, Agenda 200, Cap-reform 2003). Overall policy shifted from production support measures to income support and sustainable agriculture.

 

Concurrently attempts to cut the budget and ways to comply with international agreements were made. Adjacent policy domains affect the use of rural areas as well. From the late seventies nature policy has developed: various conventions on wildlife protection, Bird and Habitat Directive, Natura 2000. Environmental regulations relate to land use (e.g. for nutrients, pesticides). Recently the Water Directive was introduced. All in all we see a broadening in goals, a stronger interaction of policy goals and an adjustment to international agreements.

CAP: Common Agricultural Policy of European Union
Products under CAP: product with a relatively high degree of market regulation due to the Common Agricultural Policy. Products in this category are grains, oilseeds, sugar, beef and dairy.
Non-CAP products: agricultural products with a low degree of market regulation under CAP. Products in this category are horticulture, pork and poultry.

Border support: changes in export subsidies and import tariffs.
Domestic support: changes in subsidies to the farmer (e.g. area payments, slaughter premiums).


Source: Rural Development in the European Union. European Commission Agriculture and Rural Development
